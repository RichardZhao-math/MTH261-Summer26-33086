    Section 2.1 Matrix Operations

    So far, we have seen matrices popping up as we tackled systems of linear equations. Now, it is time to build our understanding from the ground up and formally define what a matrix is.
    🔗
    In this section, we will establish the core operations for matrices: addition, scalar multiplication, transposition, and matrix multiplication, as well as their properties!
    🔗

    Objectives

    After this section, students will be able to:
    🔗
    • determine the size of a matrix and identify specific entries using subscript notation.
      🔗
      🔗
    • compute the sum, scalar multiple, and transpose of given matrices.
      🔗
      🔗
    • determine if two matrices can be multiplied based on their sizes and compute their product.
      🔗
      🔗
    • apply algebraic properties to simplify matrix expressions and demonstrate why matrix multiplication is generally non-commutative.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    We actually have learned (or at least heard of) most of the matrix operations in class before. Let’s formally introduce the operations!
    🔗

    Subsection What exactly is a matrix?

    We have dealt with matrices in the last section. Let’s formally define what a matrix is since this chapter is all about matrices!
    🔗
    A matrix is a rectangular array of entries (typically numbers). The size of a matrix is determined by the number of rows and columns it has, and it is usually given in the form of \(m \times n\text{,}\) where \(m\) is the number of rows and \(n\) is the number of columns.
    🔗
    For example, the following matrix
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 4 \amp 5 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    has 2 rows and 3 columns, so its size is \(2 \times 3\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.1.1.

    What are the sizes of each of the following matrices?
    \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp -1 \amp 2 \\ -2 \amp 6 \amp 9 \\ -7 \amp 5 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix} \amp\qquad\qquad\qquad \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp -1 \amp 2 \amp 7 \\ -2 \amp 6 \amp 9 \amp 0 \\ -7 \amp 5 \amp -3 \amp -7 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \amp\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad \begin{bmatrix} \alpha \amp \beta \amp \gamma \\ \delta \amp \varepsilon \amp \zeta \\ \eta \amp \theta \amp \chi \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \\ u \\ v \end{bmatrix} \amp\qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp 4 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Basic Matrix Operations

    We will learn three operations on matrices: matrix addition, scalar multiplication, and transposition. Let’s introduce all of these operations using examples!
    🔗
    Suppose we have the matrices \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -3 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp -2 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To add two matrices together, we add all corresponding entries (those would be the things inside the brackets) and the sum is the corresponding entry in the answer.
    \begin{align*} A + B \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -3 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp -2 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 3+1 \amp 2+(-3) \amp 5+0 \\ 5+3 \amp -8+(-2) \amp 14+5 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -1 \amp 5 \\ 8 \amp -10 \amp 19 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    To multiply a scalar by a matrix, we multiply the scalar by every entry of the matrix and that product is the corresponding entry in the answer.
    \begin{align*} 7A \amp= 7 \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 7(3) \amp 7(2) \amp 7(5) \\ 7(5) \amp 7(-8) \amp 7(14) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 21 \amp 14 \amp 35 \\ 35 \amp -56 \amp 98 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    The transpose of a matrix \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(A^T\text{,}\) is obtained by flipping it across the main diagonal. That is, every row of the original matrix becomes the corresponding column of the transpose and every column of the original matrix becomes the corresponding row of the transpose.
    \begin{align*} A^T \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix}^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \\ 2 \amp -8 \\ 5 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    And we can combine all of these operations in lots of ways!
    🔗

    Example 2.1.2.

    Let’s say we want to compute \(\lp A + 2B \rp^T\text{.}\) Then we have
    \begin{align*} \lp A + 2B \rp^T \amp= \lp \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix} + 2 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -3 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp -2 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix} \rp^T \\ \amp= \lp \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -6 \amp 0 \\ 6 \amp -4 \amp 10 \end{bmatrix} \rp^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 3+2 \amp 2-6 \amp 5+0 \\ 5+6 \amp -8-4 \amp 14+10 \end{bmatrix}^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp -4 \amp 5 \\ 11 \amp -12 \amp 24 \end{bmatrix}^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 11 \\ -4 \amp -12 \\ 5 \amp 24 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.1.3.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -6 \\ 3 \amp 0 \\ 6 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \\ -5 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Compute the following (if impossible, explain why):
    1. \(\displaystyle A + B\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle B - A\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\displaystyle A + B^T\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\displaystyle (A + B)^T\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \(\displaystyle 3B\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1.
    \begin{align*} A + B \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -6 \\ 3 \amp 0 \\ 6 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \\ -5 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 2+4 \amp -6+(-2) \\ 3+(-3) \amp 0+1 \\ 6+(-5) \amp -4+(-4) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 6 \amp -8 \\ 0 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp -8 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    2.
    \begin{align*} B - A \amp= B + (-A) \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \\ -5 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp 6 \\ -3 \amp 0 \\ -6 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 4+(-2) \amp -2+6 \\ -3+(-3) \amp 1+0 \\ -5+(-6) \amp -4+4 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 4 \\ -6 \amp 1 \\ -11 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    Observe that we can just subtract the corresponding entries of \(A\) from the corresponding entries of \(B\) to get the answer. Once we have addition and scalar multiplication, we can define subtraction as adding the negatives.
    🔗
    3.
    \begin{align*} A + B^T \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -6 \\ 3 \amp 0 \\ 6 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \\ -5 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix}^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -6 \\ 3 \amp 0 \\ 6 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -3 \amp -5 \\ -2 \amp 1 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \cdots \cdots \end{align*}
    Observe that \(A\) is a \(3 \times 2\) matrix but \(B^T\) is a \(2 \times 3\) matrix. We cannot add these two matrices together because not every entry of \(A\) has a corresponding entry in \(B^T\) and vice versa.
    🔗
    4.
    \begin{align*} (A + B)^T \amp= \lp \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -6 \\ 3 \amp 0 \\ 6 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \\ -5 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} \rp^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 6 \amp -8 \\ 0 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp -8 \end{bmatrix}^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 6 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ -8 \amp 1 \amp -8 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    5.
    \begin{align*} 3B \amp= 3 \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \\ -5 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 3(4) \amp 3(-2) \\ 3(-3) \amp 3(1) \\ 3(-5) \amp 3(-4) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 12 \amp -6 \\ -9 \amp 3 \\ -15 \amp -12 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    We can make some observations about these operations:
    1. We can ONLY add two matrices when they are the exact same size. This makes sense because we do it by adding corresponding entries, so if they are different sizes, we won’t have those for all of the entries!
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(A\) is an \(m \times n\) matrix, then \(A^T\) is an \(n \times m\) matrix. This makes sense because every row of \(A\) becomes a column of \(A^T\) which also forces every column of \(A\) to become a row of \(A^T\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(A\) is called a square matrix if it has the same number of rows as columns. Notice that the only way \(A^T\) can be the same size as \(A\) is if \(A\) is a square matrix.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. When we transpose a matrix twice, we get back to the original matrix (you can "see" this if you imagine flipping it over the main diagonal twice).
      🔗
      Terminology: the main diagonal of a matrix is the one going down from left to right.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Subscript Notation for Matrices

    We can identify the location of any entry of a matrix by saying which row and column it lives in. For example, in our matrix
    \begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 2 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \amp 14 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    The entry in the second row and third column is \(14\text{.}\) We can say this in symbol as: The \((2,3)\) entry is \(14\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can also use subscript notation to denote the entries of a matrix. We indicate the row first and then the column using subscripts. For example, the \(14\) in matrix \(A\) can be denoted as \(a_{23}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A generic \(2 \times 3\) matrix would be denoted by
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    And any matrix \(A\) can be denoted in shorthand by \(\left[a_{ij}\right]\text{.}\) If your matrix is named \(C\) you would use \(c_{ij}\) to denote the entry in the \(i\)th row and the \(j\)th column.
    🔗
    Note: We always identify the row first and then the column when describing an entry of a matrix.
    🔗
    Now we can describe the operations we learned using the subscript notation!
    🔗
    Suppose \(A = [a_{ij}]\) and \(B = [b_{ij}]\text{,}\) and let \(k\) be a scalar (you can just think of a scalar as a number since in this this class they always will be). Then
    \begin{equation*} A + B = [a_{ij}] + [b_{ij}] = \left[a_{ij} + b_{ij}\right] \end{equation*}
    since we add two matrices by adding corresponding entries.
    🔗
    Similarly,
    \begin{equation*} kA = k[a_{ij}] = [ka_{ij}] \end{equation*}
    since we multiply a scalar by a matrix by multiplying it by every entry of a matrix, and
    \begin{equation*} A^T = [a_{ij}]^T = [a_{ji}] \end{equation*}
    since we transpose a matrix by turning every row into a column (and every column into a row).
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Rules for Matrix Addition and Scalar Multiplication

    Let \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) be \(m \times n\) matrices (\(m\) and \(n\) fixed), and let \(c\) and \(k\) be real numbers (scalars). Then the following properties hold:
    1. Matrix Addition is commutative. That is,
      \begin{equation*} A + B = B + A \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Matrix Addition is associative. That is,
      \begin{equation*} (A + B) + C = A + (B + C) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. There exists a zero matrix \(0\) such that
      \begin{equation*} 0 + A = A \end{equation*}
      where \(0\) is the \(m \times n\) matrix in which every entry is \(0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. For every matrix \(A\text{,}\) there exists a matrix \(-A\) such that
      \begin{equation*} A + (-A) = 0 \end{equation*}
      where \(-A\) is called the additive inverse of \(A\) and is obtained by multiplying every entry of \(A\) by \(-1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Scalar Multiplication distributes over Matrix Addition. That is,
      \begin{equation*} c(A + B) = cA + cB \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Scalar Multiplication distributes over Scalar Addition. That is,
      \begin{equation*} (c + k)A = cA + kA \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    7. Scalar Multiplication by a Product of Two Scalars is the Same as Scalar Multiplication by One Scalar Followed by Scalar Multiplication by the Other Scalar. That is,
      \begin{equation*} ck(A) = c(kA) \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    8. Scalar Multiplication by \(1\) leaves a matrix unchanged. That is,
      \begin{equation*} 1A = A \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Let’s prove them all but only in the case for \(2 \times 3\) matrices. These proofs can easily be generalized using \(\cdots\) and variables for subscripts to make the matrices completely general.
    🔗

    Proof of these Rules for \(2 \times 3\) Matrices.
    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) \(B = \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \amp b_{13} \\ b{21} \amp b_{22} \amp b_{23} \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) and \(C = \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \amp c_{13} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \amp c_{23} \end{bmatrix}\) be arbitrary \(2 \times 3\) matrices. And ket \(c\) and \(k\) be arbitrary real numbers (scalars).
    🔗
    1. (Communitivity):
    \begin{align*} A + B \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \amp b_{13} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \amp b_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} + b_{11} \amp a_{12} + b_{12} \amp a_{13} + b_{13} \\ a_{21} + b_{21} \amp a_{22} + b_{22} \amp a_{23} + b_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} + a_{11} \amp b_{12} + a_{12} \amp b_{13} + a_{13} \\ b_{21} + a_{21} \amp b_{22} + a_{22} \amp b_{23} + a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \amp b_{13} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \amp b_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= B + A \end{align*}
    🔗
    2. (Associativity):
    \begin{align*} (A + B) + C \amp= \lp \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \amp b_{13} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \amp b_{23} \end{bmatrix} \rp + \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \amp c_{13} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \amp c_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} + b_{11} \amp a_{12} + b_{12} \amp a_{13} + b_{13} \\ a_{21} + b_{21} \amp a_{22} + b_{22} \amp a_{23} + b_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \amp c_{13} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \amp c_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} (a_{11}+b_{11})+c_{11} \amp (a_{12}+b_{12})+c_{12} \amp (a_{13}+b_{13})+c_{13} \\ (a_{21}+b_{21})+c_{21} \amp (a_{22}+b_{22})+c_{22} \amp (a_{23}+b_{23})+c_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}+ (b_{11}+c_{11}) \amp a_{12}+ (b_{12}+c_{12}) \amp a_{13}+ (b_{13}+c_{13}) \\ a_{21}+ (b_{21}+c_{21}) \amp a_{22}+ (b_{22}+c_{22}) \amp a_{23}+ (b_{23}+c_{23}) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} b_{11}+c_{11} \amp b_{12}+c_{12} \amp b_{13}+c_{13}\\ b_{21}+c_{21}\amp b_{22}+c_{22}\amp b_{23}+c_{23}\end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \lp \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \amp b_{13} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \amp b_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \amp c_{13} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \amp c_{23} \end{bmatrix} \rp \\ \amp= A + (B + C) \end{align*}
    🔗
    3. (Zero Matrix):
    \begin{align*} 0 + A \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 0 + a_{11} \amp 0 + a_{12} \amp 0 + a_{13} \\ 0 + a_{21} \amp 0 + a_{22} \amp 0 + a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= A \end{align*}
    🔗
    4. (Additive Inverse):
    \begin{align*} A + (-A) \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} -a_{11} \amp -a_{12} \amp -a_{13} \\ -a_{21} \amp -a_{22} \amp -a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}+(-a_{11}) \amp a_{12}+(-a_{12}) \amp a_{13}+(-a_{13}) \\ a_{21}+(-a_{21}) \amp a_{22}+(-a_{22}) \amp a_{23}+(-a_{23}) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= 0 \end{align*}
    Then apply commutativity to get \(-A + A = 0\) (Or just do a similar proof. Either way works!)
    🔗
    5. (Distributive Part 1):
    \begin{align*} c(A + B) \amp= c \lp \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \amp b_{13} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \amp b_{23} \end{bmatrix} \rp \\ \amp= c \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} + b_{11} \amp a_{12} + b_{12} \amp a_{13} + b_{13} \\ a_{21} + b_{21} \amp a_{22} + b_{22} \amp a_{23} + b_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} c(a_{11} + b_{11}) \amp c(a_{12} + b_{12}) \amp c(a_{13} + b_{13}) \\ c(a_{21} + b_{21}) \amp c(a_{22} + b_{22}) \amp c(a_{23} + b_{23}) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} ca_{11} + cb_{11} \amp ca_{12} + cb_{12} \amp ca_{13} + cb_{13} \\ ca_{21} + cb_{21} \amp ca_{22} + cb_{22} \amp ca_{23} + cb_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} ca_{11} \amp ca_{12} \amp ca_{13} \\ ca_{21} \amp ca_{22} \amp ca_{23} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} cb_{11} \amp cb_{12} \amp cb_{13} \\ cb_{21} \amp cb_{22} \amp cb_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= cA + cB \end{align*}
    🔗
    6. (Distributive Part 2):
    \begin{align*} (c + k)A \amp= (c + k) \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} (c + k)a_{11} \amp (c + k)a_{12} \amp (c + k)a_{13} \\ (c + k)a_{21} \amp (c + k)a_{22} \amp (c + k)a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} ca_{11} + ka_{11} \amp ca_{12} + ka_{12} \amp ca_{13} + ka_{13} \\ ca_{21} + ka_{21} \amp ca_{22} + ka_{22} \amp ca_{23} + ka_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} ca_{11} \amp ca_{12} \amp ca_{13}\\ ca_{21}\amp ca_{22}\amp ca_{23}\end{bmatrix}+ \begin{bmatrix} ka_{11}\amp ka_{12}\amp ka_{13}\\ ka_{21}\amp ka_{22}\amp ka_{23}\end{bmatrix}\\ \amp= cA + kA \end{align*}
    🔗
    7. (Multiplication by Product of Scalars):
    \begin{align*} ck(A) \amp= ck \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} (ck) a_{11} \amp (ck) a_{12} \amp (ck) a_{13} \\ (ck) a_{21} \amp (ck) a_{22} \amp (ck) a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} c(ka_{11}) \amp c(ka_{12}) \amp c(ka_{13}) \\ c(ka_{21}) \amp c(ka_{22}) \amp c(ka_{23}) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= c \begin{bmatrix} ka_{11} \amp ka_{12} \amp ka_{13} \\ ka_{21} \amp ka_{22} \amp ka_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= c(kA) \end{align*}
    🔗
    8. (Scalar Multiplication by 1):
    \begin{align*} 1A \amp= 1 \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 1a_{11} \amp 1a_{12} \amp 1a_{13} \\ 1a_{21} \amp 1a_{22} \amp 1a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \amp a_{13} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \amp a_{23} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= A \end{align*}
    🔗
    Now try convincing yourself that these properties hold for matrices of any size by generalizing the above proofs!
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Rules for Transposes

    Let \(A\) and \(B\) be \(m \times n\) matrices. (Note that this notation doesn’t mean the matrices aren’t square. It just means we aren’t assuming that they are). Then the following properties hold:
    1. \(A^T\) will be an \(n \times m\) matrix.
      🔗
      If you turn every row into a column then the number of columns of the new matrix is the same as the number of rows in the original (similar with columns and rows). You can also see this by imagining flipping the matrix across its main diagonal.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\lp A^T \rp^T = A\)
      🔗
      If you turn every row into a column and then do that again, you put the matrix right back where you started. It is even more clear if you imagine reflecting the matrix across its main diagonal twice.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\lp kA \rp^T = k \lp A^T \rp\)
      🔗
      It doesn’t matter whether you (1) multiply every entry by \(k\) and then flip across the main diagonal OR (2) flip across the main diagonal and then multiply every entry by \(k\text{.}\) In either case you turn \(a_{ij}\) into \(ka_{ji}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\lp A + B \rp^T = A^T + B^T\)
      🔗
      It doesn’t matter whether you (1) add all the corresponding entries and then flip across the main diagonal OR (2) flip each matrix across the main diagonal and then add all the corresponding entries. In either case the \((i,j)\) entry is \(a_{ji} + b_{ji}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Last but not least, we call a matrix symmetric if it equals its own transpose. That is, \(A\) is symmetric if \(A = A^T\text{.}\) They are called symmetric because if you flip the matrix over the main diagonal it doesn’t change it!
    🔗
    One immediate consequence of this property is that if \(A\) is symmetric, then \(A\) must be a square matrix (or else, the two matrices won’t even be the same size, let alone equal).
    🔗
    For example, the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \end{bmatrix}\) is symmetric because
    \begin{equation*} A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \end{bmatrix}^T = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -8 \end{bmatrix} = A \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Matrix Multiplication

    Recall when you multiply a column vector by a matrix, you just compute the dot product of that vector by each row vector of the matrix. Then you get a nice column vector.
    🔗
    If you want to multiply a matrix by another matrix, you just do that for every column of the matrix on the right and you get all of the columns in your answer matrix. And that is how you multiply matrices!
    🔗

    Example 2.1.4.

    Let’s multiply the following two matrices
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp -3 \\ 0 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Oberve that the matrix on the right consists of two columns. We know how to multiply the column (aka a column vector) by the matrix. Let’s do that!
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3(2) + 5(1) + 6(0) + 2(5) \\ 1(2) + -1(1) + 2(0) + 3(5) \\ 0(2) + 5(1) + 1(0) + -1(5) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 21 \\ 16 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \\ 5 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3(1) + 5(-3) + 6(5) + 2(-2) \\ 1(1) + -1(-3) + 2(5) + 3(-2) \\ 0(1) + 5(-3) + 1(5) + -1(-2) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 14 \\ 8 \\ -8 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then the first column vector we get is the first column in the answer matrix and the second column vector we get is the second column in the answer matrix. That is,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 5 \amp 6 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 5 \amp 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp -3 \\ 0 \amp 5 \\ 5 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 21 \amp 14 \\ 16 \amp 8 \\ 0 \amp -8 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Writing out the general formula for matrix multiplication using all the little subscripts would be horrible. But there is a clever way to write matrices that makes it a bit easier.
    🔗
    Suppose you want to multiply a \(m \times n\) matrix \(A\) by the \(n\times k\) matrix \(B\text{.}\) Instead of writing down all the entries of \(B\) using double subscripts, we can write the matrix as a collection of columns using single subscripts. So the matrix \(B\) looks like this:
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} \v{b}_1 \amp \v{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \v{b}_k \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then
    \begin{align*} AB \amp= A \begin{bmatrix} \v{b}_1 \amp \v{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \v{b}_k \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} A\v{b}_1 \amp A\v{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp A\v{b}_k \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    where each column of the answer is a product of \(A\) with the corresponding column vector of \(B\text{.}\) And each of those products are computed by taking the dot product of each row of \(A\) with that column vector.
    🔗
    Summary: the \((i,j)\) entry of the matrix \(AB\) is computed by taking the dot product of the \(i\)th row of \(A\) with the \(j\)-th column of \(B\) (rows times columns).
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.1.5.

    Multiply! If it is impossible, say why!
    1. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 5 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp -3 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 5 \amp -2 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 5 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp -3 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 1\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 3 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 7 \amp 2 \\ 5 \amp -2 \amp -2 \\ 7 \amp 8 \amp 9 \end{bmatrix} \)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1.
    \begin{align*} \amp \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 5 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp -3 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \\ 5 \amp -2 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \\ = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 1(2) + 5(1) + 2(5) \amp 1(-1) + 5(3) + 2(-2) \amp 1(0) + 5(2) + 2(0) \\ 3(2) + 2(1) + 1(5) \amp 3(-1) + 2(3) + 1(-2) \amp 3(0) + 2(2) + 1(0) \\ 0(2) + 2(1) + -3(5) \amp 0(-1) + 2(3) + -3(-2) \amp 0(0) + 2(2) + -3(0) \\ 1(2) + 1(1) + 1(5) \amp 1(-1) + 1(3) + 1(-2) \amp 1(0) + 1(2) + 1(0) \end{bmatrix} \\ = \amp \begin{bmatrix} 17 \amp 10 \amp 10 \\ 13 \amp 1 \amp 4 \\ -13 \amp 12 \amp 4 \\ 8 \amp 0 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    2. This is impossible. You can’t multiply a \(4 \times 3\) matrix by a \(4 \times 3\) matrix. None of the dot products work!
    🔗
    For example, if you want to find the \((1,1)\) entry in the answer matrix, then you would find the dot product of the first row of the matrix to the left and the first column of the matrix to the right. But... there are three entries in the first row vector and four entries in the column vector... The sizes don’t work out... (there is nothing we can multiply by the \(7\))...
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    To make sure the dot products work out for matrix multiplication, we want to make sure the number of columns in the first matrix matches up with the number of rows in the second matrix. That is, to multiply two matrices \(A\) and \(B\) with the size of \(A\) being \(m\times n\text{,}\) the size of \(B\) must be \(n \times k\text{.}\) The answer matrix is of the size of \(m\times k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    New operation means more rules! Here is a theorem that collects some rules about matrix multiplication.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.1.6. Matrix Multiplication Rules.

    Suppose \(A\text{,}\) \(B\text{,}\) and \(C\) are matrices that are the right size for the products to be defined. And suppose \(I\) is the identity matrix that is the right size as well. The following properties hold:
    1. Identity Property: \(IA = A\) and \(AI = A\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Associativity: \(A(BC) = (AB)C\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. (Left) Distributive Property: \(A(B + C) = AB + AC\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. (Right) Distributive Property: \((A + B)C = AC + BC\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Regrouping with Scalar Multiplication; For any scalar \(c\text{,}\) \(c(AB) = (cA)B = A(cB)\)
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Transpose of a Product: \((AB)^T = B^T A^T\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The first five rules should not be surprising. Spend some time to convince yourself why they are true!
    🔗

    Proof Ideas and Sketches Using Smaller Matrices.
    1. The first equation follows from multiplying the identify matrix by a column vector. You get that same column vector as the answer. Since that would happen for every column of \(A\text{,}\) you will get \(A\) as your answer.
      🔗
      The second equation is true because when you multiply \(A\) by the \(k\)th column of \(I\text{,}\) the single \(1\) in the \(k\)th row of that column will reproduce the \(k\)th column of the matrix.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. This one gets messy when the matrices start getting bigger, so let’s verify it in the \(2 \times 2\) case. The proof in general works the same just with a lot more subscripts to juggle!
      \begin{align*} A(BC) \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \lp \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \rp \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_{11}c_{11} + b_{12}c_{21} \amp b_{11}c_{12} + b_{12}c_{22} \\ b_{21}c_{11} + b_{22}c_{21} \amp b_{21}c_{12} + b_{22}c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}\lp b_{11}c_{11} + b_{12}c_{21}\rp + a_{12} \lp b_{21}c_{11} + b_{22}c_{21} \rp \amp a_{11}\lp b_{11}c_{12} + b_{12}c_{22}\rp + a_{12}\lp b_{21}c_{12} + b_{22}c_{22}\rp \\ a_{21}\lp b_{11}c_{11} + b_{12}c_{21}\rp + a_{22}\lp b_{21}c_{11} + b_{22}c_{21}\rp \amp a_{21}\lp b_{11}c_{12} + b_{12}c_{22}\rp + a_{22}\lp b_{21}c_{12} + b_{22}c_{22}\rp \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}b_{11}c_{11} + a_{11}b_{12}c_{21} + a_{12}b_{21}c_{11} + a_{12}b_{22}c_{21} \amp a_{11}b_{11}c_{12} + a_{11}b_{12}c_{22} + a_{12}b_{21}c_{12} + a_{12}b_{22}c_{22} \\ a_{21}b_{11}c_{11} + a_{21}b_{12}c_{21} + a_{22}b_{21}c_{11} + a_{22}b_{22}c_{21} \amp a_{21}b_{11}c_{12} + a_{21}b_{12}c_{22} + a_{22}b_{21}c_{12} + a_{22}b_{22}c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      and
      \begin{align*} (AB)C \amp= \lp \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \end{bmatrix} \rp \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}b_{11} + a_{12}b_{21} \amp a_{11}b_{12} + a_{12}b_{22} \\ a_{21}b_{11} + a_{22}b_{21} \amp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{22}b_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} \lp a_{11}b_{11} + a_{12}b_{21} \rp c_{11} + \lp a_{11}b_{12} + a_{12}b_{22} \rp c_{21} \amp \lp a_{11}b_{11} + a_{12}b_{21} \rp c_{12} + \lp a_{11}b_{12} + a_{12}b_{22} \rp c_{22} \\ \lp a_{21}b_{11} + a_{22}b_{21} \rp c_{11} + \lp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{22}b_{22} \rp c_{21} \amp \lp a_{21}b_{11} + a_{22}b_{21} \rp c_{12} + \lp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{22}b_{22} \rp c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}b_{11}c_{11} + a_{12}b_{21}c_{11} + a_{11}b_{12}c_{21} + a_{12}b_{22}c_{21} \amp a_{11}b_{11}c_{12} + a_{12}b_{21}c_{12} + a_{11}b_{12}c_{22} + a_{12}b_{22}c_{22} \\ a_{21}b_{11}c_{11} + a_{22}b_{21}c_{11} + a_{21}b_{12}c_{21} + a_{22}b_{22}c_{21} \amp a_{21}b_{11}c_{12} + a_{22}b_{21}c_{12} + a_{21}b_{12}c_{22} + a_{22}b_{22}c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
      Comparing the results, we can see that the matrix multiplication is associative because multiplication of numbers is!
      🔗
      🔗
    3. This one isn’t quite as messy as the previous one, but still warrants limiting to \(2 \times 2\) case:
      \begin{align*} A(B + C) \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \lp \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \rp \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} + c_{11} \amp b_{12} + c_{12} \\ b_{21} + c_{21} \amp b_{22} + c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}\lp b_{11} + c_{11}\rp + a_{12}\lp b_{21} + c_{21}\rp \amp a_{11}\lp b_{12} + c_{12}\rp + a_{12}\lp b_{22} + c_{22}\rp \\ a_{21}\lp b_{11} + c_{11}\rp + a_{22}\lp b_{21} + c_{21}\rp \amp a_{21}\lp b_{12} + c_{12}\rp + a_{22}\lp b_{22} + c_{22}\rp \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}b_{11} + a_{11}c_{11} + a_{12}b_{21} + a_{12}c_{21} \amp a_{11}b_{12} + a_{11}c_{12} + a_{12}b_{22} + a_{12}c_{22} \\ a_{21}b_{11} + a_{21}c_{11} + a_{22}b_{21} + a_{22}c_{21} \amp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{21}c_{12} + a_{22}b_{22} + a_{22}c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} \lp a_{11}b_{11} + a_{12}b_{21}\rp + \lp a_{11}c_{11} + a_{12}c_{21}\rp \amp \lp a_{11}b_{12} + a_{12}b_{22}\rp + \lp a_{11}c_{12} + a_{12}c_{22}\rp \\ \lp a_{21}b_{11} + a_{22}b_{21}\rp + \lp a_{21}c_{11} + a_{22}c_{21}\rp \amp \lp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{22}b_{22}\rp + \lp a_{21}c_{12} + a_{22}c_{22}\rp \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}b_{11} + a_{12}b_{21} \amp a_{11}b_{12} + a_{12}b_{22} \\ a_{21}b_{11} + a_{22}b_{21} \amp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{22}b_{22} \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}c_{11} + a_{12}c_{21} \amp a_{11}c_{12} + a_{12}c_{22} \\ a_{21}c_{11} + a_{22}c_{21} \amp a_{21}c_{12} + a_{22}c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \lp \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \end{bmatrix} \rp + \lp \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_{11} \amp c_{12} \\ c_{21} \amp c_{22} \end{bmatrix} \rp \\ \amp= AB + AC \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The verification of this one works just like the previous one with the obvious changes. Again it comes down to the fact that we have the distributive property in basic arithmetic.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. I encourage you to verify this for the \(2\times 2\) case. This one is much less messy than the ones we have done!
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    You may find the last rule a bit surprising. Why isn’t \((AB)^T\) equal to \(A^T B^T\text{?}\)
    🔗
    This surprise should tell you that matrix multiplication is not commutative!
    🔗
    Let’s verify this one for the \(2 \times 2\) case!
    \begin{align*} (AB)^T \amp= \lp \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \end{bmatrix} \rp^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}b_{11} + a_{12}b_{21} \amp a_{11}b_{12} + a_{12}b_{22} \\ a_{21}b_{11} + a_{22}b_{21} \amp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{22}b_{22} \end{bmatrix}^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} a_{11}b_{11} + a_{12}b_{21} \amp a_{21}b_{11} + a_{22}b_{21} \\ a_{11}b_{12} + a_{12}b_{22} \amp a_{21}b_{12} + a_{22}b_{22} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    If you computed \(A^T B^T\) and compare it to this you would find you would NOT get this result! But if we commute all of the products inside this matrix...
    \begin{align*} \amp \qquad \, \, \, = \begin{bmatrix} b_{11}a_{11} + b_{21}a_{12} \amp b_{11}a_{21} + b_{21}a_{22} \\ b_{12}a_{11} + b_{22}a_{12} \amp b_{12}a_{21} + b_{22}a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    This is what you would get for \(B^T A^T\text{!}\)
    \begin{align*} B^T A^T \amp= \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{12} \\ b_{21} \amp b_{22} \end{bmatrix}^T \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{12} \\ a_{21} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix}^T \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} b_{11} \amp b_{21} \\ b_{12} \amp b_{22} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \amp a_{21} \\ a_{12} \amp a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} b_{11}a_{11} + b_{21}a_{12} \amp b_{11}a_{21} + b_{21}a_{22} \\ b_{12}a_{11} + b_{22}a_{12} \amp b_{12}a_{21} + b_{22}a_{22} \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    🔗
    Observe that commutativity is not included in the theorem. That is because matrix multiplication is NOT commutative, which means in general it is NOT true that \(AB = BA\text{.}\) Sometimes it happens that way but often it does not!
    🔗
    A quick counter-example can show that matrix multiplication is non-commutative. Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Observe that
    \begin{equation*} AB = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    but
    \begin{equation*} BA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -2 \\ 1 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(AB \neq BA\text{...}\)
