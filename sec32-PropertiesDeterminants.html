    Section 3.2 Properties of Determinants

    In this section, we will see how to compute the determinant of a matrix inverse, which also tells us about the invertibility of a matrix.
    🔗

    Objectives

    After this section, students will be able to:
    🔗
    • Apply the multiplicative property of determinants
      🔗
      🔗
    • determine whether a matrix is invertible by computing its determinant
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The most important theorem about determinants is this one:
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.1. Determinants and Products.

    Suppose \(A\) and \(B\) are \(n \times n\) matrices. Then
    \begin{equation*} \det (AB) = \det A \det B \end{equation*}
    That is, the determinant of the product is the product of the determinants.
    🔗
    🔗
    And one consequence of this is the following Lemma:
    🔗

    Lemma 3.2.2.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix, then \(\det A = 0\) if and only if \(A\) has no inverse (hence \(A\) is not invertible).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.2.3.

    We know that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp -2 \\ -9 \amp 5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 0 \\ -9 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp \frac{-2}{5} \\ 0 \amp \frac{7}{5} \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    We can calculate the determinant of all three matrices and quickly verify that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{vmatrix} 5 \amp -2 \\ -9 \amp 5 \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 5 \amp 0 \\ -9 \amp 1 \end{vmatrix} \begin{vmatrix} 1 \amp \frac{-2}{5} \\ 0 \amp \frac{7}{5} \end{vmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    If we know that \(A\) is invertible, then we can use the above theorem to compute the determinant of the inverse of \(A\text{.}\) Since \(AA^{-1} = I\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} \det (AA^{-1}) \amp= \det I \\ \implies \qquad \det A \det A^{-1} \amp= 1 \\ \implies\qquad \qquad \det A^{-1} \amp= \frac{1}{\det A} \end{align*}
    Hence, \(\det\lp A^{-1} \rp = \dfrac{1}{\det A}\text{.}\) We can make it a theorem!
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.4. Determinants and Inverses.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) invertible matrix. Then
    \begin{equation*} \det A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    The next theorem tells us about the determinants and transpose.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.5. Determinants and Transpose.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix. Then
    \begin{equation*} \det A^T = \det A \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    What the theorem tells us is that the determinant of any square matrix is the same as the determinant of its transpose.
    🔗

    Proof using Elementary Matrices....
    First, let’s make sure this is true for elementary matrices.
    1. If \(E\) is obtained by swapping two rows on \(I\text{,}\) then \(E^T\) is obtained by swapping the corresponding columns of \(I\text{.}\) In each case, the determinant is \(-1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(E\) is obtained by multiplying a row of \(I\) by a constant, then notice that \(E^T = E\text{.}\) So certainly \(E^T\) and \(E\) have the same determinant in this case (it is whatever the constant is).
      🔗
      🔗
    3. If \(E\) is obtained by adding a multiple of a row of \(I\) to another row of \(I\text{.}\) The transpose could be obtained the same way (reversing the two rows). Or we we could do it by doing the same operation on the corresponding columns of \(I\text{.}\) In any case, the determinant of \(E\) and \(E^T\) are both \(1\) because those operations do not change the determinant of \(I\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    So the result holds for elementary matrices.
    🔗
    Now let’s do the proof for any \(n \times n\) matrix \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(A\) is not invertible, then neither is \(A^T\text{.}\)

    Note 3.2.6. Hmm why is that?

    If \(A^T B = I\text{,}\) then \(B^T A = I^T = I\text{.}\) So we would have an inverse for \(A\) if we had one for \(A^T\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    So if \(\det A = 0\text{,}\) then \(\det A^T = 0\) as well.
    🔗
    Suppose \(\det A \neq 0\text{,}\) then \(A\) is invertible and can be written as a product of elementary matrices. Let’s say
    \begin{equation*} A = E_1 E_2 \ldots E_k \end{equation*}
    Then by this theorem Theorem 2.1.6, we know that
    \begin{equation*} A^T = E_K^T \ldots E_2^T E_1^T \end{equation*}
    Therefore,
    \begin{align*} \det A^T \amp= \det \lp E_K^T \ldots E_2^T E_1^T \rp \\ \amp= \det E_k^T \, \ldots \, \det E_2^T \, \det E_1^T \\ \amp= \det E_k \, \ldots \, \det E_2 \, \det E_1 \\ \amp= \det E_1 \, \det E_2 \, \ldots \, \det E_K \\ \amp= \det \lp E_1 E_2 \ldots E_k \rp \\ \amp= \det A \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof Idea using Mathematical Induction....
    This proof (idea) is easier but uses a proof technique most MTH 261 students would not be familiar with called Mathematical Induction.
    🔗
    The idea in this case is that the theory is easily verified for \(2 \times 2\) matrices:
    • If \(A = \begin{bmatrix} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) we can see that
      \begin{equation*} \det A = ad - bc \end{equation*}
      Since \(A^T = \begin{bmatrix} a \amp c \\ b \amp d \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) we can verify that
      \begin{equation*} \det A^T = ad - bc \end{equation*}
      Same thing.
      🔗
      🔗
    Now suppose we were working with a \(3 \times 3\) matrix. Then the first row of \(A\) becomes the first column of \(A^T\text{.}\) So if we calculate the determinant using the cofactor method and expand on the first row of \(A\) and the first column of \(A^T\text{,}\) we will see that we get the same thing except each of the little \(2 \times 2\) determinants has been transposed when we are working with \(A^T\text{.}\) And since we know from the \(2 \times 2\) case that these are the same, it follows that we get the same answer for \(\det A\) and \(\det A^T\) in the \(3 \times 3\) case as well.
    🔗
    Then we can repeat that same strategy for \(4 \times 4\) and so on and so on forever and ever...
    🔗
    🔗
    In the 4th lab report, you will explore an application of the determinant, which is to extend the inverse formula back in Section 2.2using determinants and adjugates.
