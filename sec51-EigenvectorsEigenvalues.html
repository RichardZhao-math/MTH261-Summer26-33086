    Section 4.1 Eigenvectors and Eigenvalues

    Investigation 4.1.1.

    Suppose \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp -2 \\ 1 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(T: \R^2 \to \R^2\) be a linear transformation defined by \(T(\v{x}) = A\v{x}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    (a)

    Let \(\v{u} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Compute \(T(\v{u})\text{,}\) and then sketch the vectors \(\v{u}\) and \(T(\v{u})\text{.}\) What is their relationship?
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Let \(\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Compute \(T(\v{v})\text{,}\) and then sketch the vectors \(\v{v}\) and \(T(\v{v})\text{.}\) What is their relationship?
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In the previous investigation, we found that \(\v{v}\) and \(T(\v{v})\) are parallel, which allows us to express \(T(\v{v})\) as a scalar multiple of the preimage \(\v{v}\text{.}\) Observe that matrix-vector multiplication is hard. But scalar multiplication of a vector is much easier. This is the idea behind eigenvalues and eigenvectors, which allows us to turn a matrix-vector multiplication to a scalar multiplication!
    🔗

    Definition 4.1.1. Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix. An eigenvector of \(A\) is a nonzero vector \(\v{x}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} A\v{x} = \lambda \v{x} \end{equation*}
    for some scalar \(\lambda\text{.}\) Such a scalar \(\lambda\) is called an eigenvalue of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We call \(\v{x}\) here an eigenvector corresponding to the eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Note 4.1.2. But Richard... Why can’t we allow the zero vector to be an eigenvector?

    Great question! Let’s do a quick experiment by allowing the zero vector to be an eigenvector.
    🔗
    If \(\v{0}\) is an eigenvector of some square matrix \(A\text{,}\) then we have
    \begin{equation*} A\v{0} = \lambda \v{0} \end{equation*}
    for some scalar \(\lambda\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then what are the eigenvalues of this matrix \(A\text{?}\)
    🔗
    The answer is every scalar! This is because the equation
    \begin{equation*} A\v{0} = \lambda \v{0} \end{equation*}
    is always true regardless of the value of \(\lambda\) (we ended up getting \(\v{0} = \v{0}\)). Then why bother defining the concept of eigenvalues of a matrix if every scalar is an eigenvalue?
    🔗
    Observe that allowing the zero vector to be an eigenvector will trivialize the concept of eigenvalues. So we exclude the zero vector from being an eigenvector.
    🔗
    🔗
    The goal of this section is to be able to find the eigenvalues and its corresponding eigenvectors of a matrix. Let’s do some verification, and then we will summarize the process that can be applied to any square matrix.
    🔗

    Example 4.1.3.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 6 \\ 5 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Is \(\v{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix}\) an eigenvector of \(A\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Based on the definition, an eigenvector can turn a matrix-vector multiplication into a scalar multiplication. So let’s compute \(A\v{u}\) and see if we can express it as a scalar multiple of \(\v{u}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Observe that
    \begin{equation*} A\v{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 6 \\ 5 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -24 \\ 20 \end{bmatrix} = -4 \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix} = -4\v{u} \end{equation*}
    So we can express this matrix-vector multiplication as scaling the vector by a factor of \(-4\text{.}\) Hence, \(\v{u}\) is an eigenvector of \(A\text{,}\) and the corresponding eigenvalue is \(-4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    What about \(\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}\text{?}\) Is \(\v{v}\) also an eigenvector of \(A\text{?}\)
    🔗
    Let’s do the same thing as before by computing \(A\v{v}\) and see if we can express it as a scalar multiple of \(\v{v}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} A\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 6 \\ 5 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -9 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\begin{bmatrix} -9 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix}\) is not a scalar multiple of \(\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) the vector \(\v{v}\) is not an eigenvector of \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Richard claims that an eigenvalue of \(A\) is \(7\text{.}\) How can we verify this claim?
    🔗
    If \(\lambda = 7\text{,}\) then we can plug this value into the equation \(A\v{x} = \lambda \v{x}\) and be able to find the corresponding eigenvectors. So let’s first plug in \(\lambda = 7\) into the equation \(A\v{x} = \lambda \v{x}\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 6 \\ 5 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \v{x} = 7\v{x} \end{equation*}
    We can assume \(\v{x} = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}\) and rewrite the equation as
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 6 \\ 5 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = 7 \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    This is equivalent to the system of equations
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} a + 6b = 7a \\ 5a + 2b = 7b \end{cases} \qquad \implies \qquad \begin{cases} (1 - 7)a + 6b = 0 \\ 5a + (2 - 7)b = 0 \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Now we can solve this system of equations using augmented matrices:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 - 7 \amp 6 \amp \mid \amp 0 \\ 5 \amp 2 - 7 \amp \mid \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -6 \amp 6 \amp \mid \amp 0 \\ 5 \amp -5 \amp \mid \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Converting this matrix to row echelon form, we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \amp \mid \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \mid \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    This implies that \(a - b = 0\) and \(b\) is a free variable. Hence, the corresponding eigenvectors are of the form \(\v{x} = \begin{bmatrix} b \\ b \end{bmatrix} = b \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) for any real number of \(b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Suppose \(\v{x}\) is an eigenvector of \(A\text{.}\) Then \(\v{x}\) must be a solution to the equation \(A\v{x} = \lambda \v{x}\) for some scalar \(\lambda\text{.}\) If we do some quick algebraic manipulation, we can obtain the following equation
    \begin{equation*} A\v{x} - \lambda \v{x} = \v{0} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(\v{x}\) is a common factor of the left-hand side, so we can factor it out and rewrite the equation as
    \begin{equation*} (A - \lambda)\v{x} = \v{0} \end{equation*}
    Hmm... But this equation doesn’t quick make sense... What is "\(A - \lambda\)"? That is, how can we subtract a scalar from a matrix?
    🔗
    From the previous example, we saw that \(7\) is an eigenvalue of \(A\text{,}\) and we ended up subtracting \(7\) from the diagonal entries of \(A\text{.}\) So instead of writing \(A - \lambda\text{,}\) which makes no sense, we can rewrite it as \(A - \lambda I\text{,}\) where \(I\) is the identity matrix. Hence, we can rewrite the equation as
    \begin{equation*} (A - \lambda I)\v{x} = \v{0} \end{equation*}
    Now the equation makes sense! This is just a homogeneous equation so the solution (aka the eigenvectors) is captured by the null space of the matrix \(A - \lambda I\text{.}\) Finding the eigenvectors of a matrix corresponding to a matrix is equivalent to finding the null space of the matrix \(A - \lambda I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Be careful! Recall that we don’t allow the zero vector to be an eigenvector. Hence, we want to find the non-trivial solutions to the equation \((A - \lambda I)\v{x} = \v{0}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 4.1.4.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -1 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 8 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) An eigenvalue of this matrix is \(2\text{.}\) Let’s find the corresponding eigenvectors!
    🔗
    Essentially we want to find the non-trivial solutions to the equation \((A - 2I)\v{x} = \v{0}\text{.}\) So let’s find the matrix \(A - 2I\) first:
    \begin{equation*} A - 2I = \begin{bmatrix} 4 - 2 \amp -1 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp 1 - 2 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 8 - 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Now we can find the null space of the matrix \(A - 2I\) by solving the homogeneous equation \((A - 2I)\v{x} = \v{0}\) using the augmented matrix:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \amp 6 \amp \mid \amp 0 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 6 \amp \mid \amp 0 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 6 \amp \mid \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \amp 3 \amp \mid \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \mid \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \mid \amp 0\end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) are free variables, and \(x_1 = \frac{1}{2}x_2 - 3x_3\text{.}\) Then we can express the solution, in parametric form, as
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2}x_2 - 3x_3 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = x_2 \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_3 \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then the corresponding eigenvectors are of the form \(\v{x} = x_2 \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_3 \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) for any real numbers of \(x_2\) and \(x_3\text{,}\) except for the case when both \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) are zero.
    🔗
    🔗
    Observe that all the eigenvectors corresponding to a specific eigenvalue of a matrix will live in \(\Nul(A - \lambda I)\text{,}\) which is a subspace. Then we can define eigenspace as the null space of the matrix \(A - \lambda I\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 4.1.5. Eigenspace.

    Let \(A\) be an \(n \times n\) matrix, and let \(\lambda\) be an eigenvalue of \(A\text{.}\) The eigenspace of \(A\) corresponding to the eigenvalue \(\lambda\text{,}\) denoted by \(E_\lambda\text{,}\) is the null space of the matrix \(A - \lambda I\text{.}\) Symbolically speaking,
    \begin{equation*} E_\lambda = \Nul(A - \lambda I) \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    It is correct to say that the eigenspace includes all the eigenvectors corresponding to the eigenvalue of a matrix, but it is incorrect to say that everything in the eigenspace is an eigenvector. Remember that the zero vector is always in the subspace, but it is not an eigenvector. So eigenspace is essentially the set of all eigenvectors corresponding to a specific eigenvalue of a matrix, together with the zero vector.
    🔗

    Example 4.1.6.

    Back to our previous example of \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp -1 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp -1 \amp 8 \end{bmatrix}\) with an eigenvalue of \(2\text{,}\) we can find its eigenspace \(E_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We did all the heavy-lifting work in the previous example by solving the homogeneous equation \(A - 2I = \v{0}\text{,}\) with the solution, in parametric form, as
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2}x_2 - 3x_3 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = x_2 \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_3 \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then the eigenspace \(E_2\text{,}\) aka \(\Nul(A - 2I)\text{,}\) is the span of those two basis vectors. That is,
    \begin{equation*} E_2 = \span\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\} \end{equation*}
