    Section 2.5 Dimension and Rank

    In the previous section, we explored how to build a subspace using a spanning set. But this raises an important question for us to investigate: are all the vectors in a spanning set actually necessary? Sometimes, a set contains "redundant" vectors that don’t add any new building blocks to the space.
    🔗
    In this section, we are going to explore the concepts of linear independence and dimension.
    🔗

    Objectives

    After this section, students will be able to:
    🔗
    • construct a basis for a subspace.
      🔗
      🔗
    • determine the dimension of a subspace.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Dimensions

    Recall that the number of vectors in a basis is a finite number for a given subspace, and we can generate the whole subspace by taking linear combinations of the vectors in the basis. Then we define the dimension of a subspace as the number of vectors in a basis for that subspace.
    🔗

    Definition 2.5.1. Dimension of a Subspace of \(\R^n\).

    Let \(U\) be a subspace and \(\set{\v{v}_1, \v{v}_2, \ldots, \v{v}_p}\) be a basis of \(U\text{.}\) Then the dimension of \(U\text{,}\) denoted \(\dim U\text{,}\) is the number of vectors in the basis, which is \(p\text{.}\) That is, \(\dim U = p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.5.2.

    It makes sense to say that the dimension of \(\R^2\) is \(2\) and the dimension of \(\R^3\) is \(3\text{.}\) But how does it match with our definition of dimension?
    🔗
    We can verify this by finding a basis for \(\R^2\) and \(\R^3\) easily. Earlier, we saw that \(\left\{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\}\) is a basis for \(\R^2\text{,}\) so \(\dim \R^2 = 2\text{.}\) The basis is called the standard basis for \(\R^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Similarly, \(\left\{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\}\) is a basis for \(\R^3\text{,}\) so \(\dim \R^3 = 3\text{.}\) This basis is called the standard basis for \(\R^3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    More generally, the dimension of \(\R^n\) is \(n\) since the standard basis for \(\R^n\) is
    \begin{equation*} \left\{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ \vdots \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \ldots, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ \vdots \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\} \end{equation*}
    and there are exactly \(n\) vectors in this basis.
    🔗
    🔗
    Recall we talked about two fundamental subspaces associated with a matrix in the last section: the null space and the column space. We learned how to find a basis for these two subspaces in the last section. Now we can find the dimension of them as well!
    🔗
    To find the dimension, we need to first find a basis for the subspace (specific instruction is in the last section!). Then we can just count the number of vectors in the basis to find the dimension.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.3.

    Let’s say we have the matrix \(A = \begin{bmatrix} -3 \amp 6 \amp -1 \amp 1 \amp 7 \\ 1 \amp -2 \amp 2 \amp 3 \amp -1 \\ 2 \amp -4 \amp 5 \amp 8 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix}\) and this time we want to find a basis for the null space and the column space.
    🔗
    In the last section, we found the null space and the column space of the matrix \(A\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \Nul A \amp= \span\left\{\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -2 \\ 1 \\ 0\end{bmatrix}\right\} \\ \Col A \amp= \span\left\{\begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ -1 \\ -4 \end{bmatrix}\right\} \end{align*}
    Observe that there are two vectors in the basis for the null space and three vectors in the basis for the column space. Hence,
    \begin{equation*} \dim\Nul A = 2 \qquad \text{ and } \qquad \dim\Col A = 3 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.5.4.

    Let \(B = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \amp -2 \amp -4 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Find a basis and the dimension for \(\Nul B\) and \(\Col B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We will need to determine whether the columns of \(B\) contains a pivot position or not, so a good first step is to row reduce the matrix.
    \begin{equation*} B = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \amp -2 \amp -4 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 2 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 6 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 2 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that the first three columns contains pivot positions, so we can span the whole column space using these three columns. That is,
    \begin{equation*} \Col B = \span \left\{ \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\} \end{equation*}
    Observe that there are three vectors in the basis, so \(\dim \Col B = 3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    To find a basis for the null space, we essentially need to solve the homogeneous equation \(B\v{x} = \v{0}\text{.}\) Since the constant column of zeros is not affected by row operations, we can use the row reduced form of \(B\) to solve the homogeneous equation.
    🔗
    Since there is no pivot position in the last two columns, \(x_4\) and \(x_5\) are free variables. Hence, we can express \(x_1\text{,}\) \(x_2\text{,}\) and \(x_3\) in terms of \(x_4\) and \(x_5\) as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} x_1 = s - 2t \\ x_2 = -2s + t \\ x_3 = -s - 3t \\ x_4 = s \\ x_5 = t \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    So we can describe the solution in the parametric form as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = s\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + t\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ -3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Hence, the null space is spanned by the above two basis vectors. That is,
    \begin{equation*} \Nul B = \span\left\{ \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \\ -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ -3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \right\} \end{equation*}
    Observe that there are two vectors in the basis, so \(\dim \Nul B = 2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection A Fundamental Theorem of Linear Algebra: Rank-Nullity Theorem

    You may have noticed a pattern between the dimension of the null space and the dimension of a column space through the problems: we form a basis of the column space by taking the pivot columns and can construct a basis of the null space by focusing on the non-pivot columns. That is, we used every single columns when constructing the basis of the column space and the dimension of the null space. Their dimensions should add back up to the number of columns!
    🔗
    Terminology! There are special terms to describe the dimensions here:
    • The dimension of the null space is called the nullity.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The dimension of the column space is called the rank.
      🔗
      🔗
    A big theorem to connect the dimension of these two fundamental subspaces is called the Rank-Nullity Theorem! This is considered a fundamental theorem of linear algebra as it connects two fundamental subspaces in linear algebra together!
    🔗

    Theorem 2.5.5. Rank-Nullity Theorem.

    Let \(A\) be a \(m \times n\) matrix, then
    \begin{equation*} \Rank A + \Nullity A = n \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    You will explore in your last lab report the other fundamental theorems of linear algebra as there are two more fundamental subspaces (the row space and the left null space) that we have not discussed yet, and there are other interesting relationships between them in addition to the dimensions!
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Extending Our Invertible Matrix Theorem!

    Back in Section 2.3 we saw our first version of the Invertible Matrix Theorem (Theorem 2.3.1).
    🔗
    Now that we learn more fancy things in linear algebra, we can add a couple more statements that are equivalent to the twelve statements in the theorem so far:
    • 13. The columns of \(A\) form a basis of \(\R^n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • 14. \(\Col A = \R^n\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • 15. \(\dim \Col A = n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • 16. \(\Nul A = \set{\v{0}}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • 17. \(\dim \Nul A = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    They should be immediate results from the Invertible Matrix Theorem, but rephrasing it using all the fancy concepts we learned in this chapter.
    🔗
    We will continue building on this theorem as we learn more concepts in linear algebra, so stay tuned!
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Coordinates System

    In \(\R^2\text{,}\) the standard basis is \(\set{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}}\text{.}\) But what makes this the STANDARD basis?
    🔗
    That is because we build our \(\R^2\) coordinate system using this basis (so this is like our default setting in \(\R^2\) coordinate system).
    🔗
    Observe that the two standard basis vectors \(\v{e}_1\) and \(\v{e}_2\) not only define the two directions of the coordinate system (so they can’t be parallel!) but also what the unit length looks like in both directions. Then we can represent any vector in \(\R^2\) as a linear combination of these two standard basis vectors, and the coordinates are the scalars in such a linear combination.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.7.

    Consider our standard basis \(\set{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}}\) in \(\R^2\text{.}\) How can we represent the vector \(\v{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}\) using this basis?
    🔗
    We can write \(\v{x}\) as a linear combination of the two standard basis vectors as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = 3\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + 5\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    That is, we can graph this vector \(\v{x}\) by moving 3 units in the direction of \(\v{e}_1\) and 5 units in the direction of \(\v{e}_2\) (then add these two vectors together).
    🔗
    So it makes sense to say that the coordinate of the vector \(\v{x}\) relative to the standard basis is \(\begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Since we can find a different basis for a subspace, then we can also build a different coordinate system using a different basis. Once you do that, then the coordinates of a vector will change as well. That is, the coordinate of a vector depends on the basis we choose to build the coordinate system.
    🔗

    Example 2.5.9.

    Let’s use a different basis to build the coordinate system in \(\R^2\text{.}\) Now let’s pick the set \(\mathcal{B} = \set{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix}}\) to be a basis for \(\R^2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can quickly verify that these two vectors are linearly independent and spans \(\R^2\text{,}\) so they form a basis for \(\R^2\).
    🔗
    Then we can create a new coordinate system using this basis! Just for notational briefness, let’s call the first basis vector \(\v{b}_1\) and the second basis vector \(\v{b}_2\text{.}\) Then our new coordinate system looks like this below:
    🔗
    To give you a better idea of what the new coordinate system looks like, Richard removed the standard coordinate system and also added the grid lines to help you visualize the new coordinate system.
    🔗
    Next, let’s say we want to find the new coordinate of the vector \(\v{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}\) using this new coordinate system. First, let’s take a look at the graph of the vector \(\v{x}\) in the new coordinate system:
    🔗
    Observe that the vector \(\v{x}\) can be obtained in the new coordinate system by moving 2 units backwards in the direction of \(\v{b}_1\) and 1 unit forward in the direction of \(\v{b}_2\text{.}\) So the new coordinate of the vector \(\v{x}\) relative to the basis \(\mathcal{B}\) is \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can also find the scalars by figuring out the linear combination of the two basis vectors that gives us the vector \(\v{x}\text{.}\) That is,
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = -2\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -2 \end{bmatrix} + 1\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ -1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    So the new coordinate of the vector \(\v{x}\) relative to the basis \(\mathcal{B}\) is \(\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fancy Notation! The notation of the new coordinate is \([\v{x}]_\mathcal{B} = \begin{bmatrix} - 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Of course, this idea can be extended to any subspace of \(\R^n\text{!}\) Let’s wrap up this section by summarizing this idea into a fancy definition!
    🔗

    Definition 2.5.13.

    Let \(\mathcal{B} = \set{\v{b}_1, \v{b}_2, \ldots, \v{b}_p}\) be a subspace \(H\) of \(\R^n\text{.}\) For each \(\v{x} \in H\text{,}\) the coordinate of \(\v{x}\) relative to \(\mathcal{B}\) are the scalars \(c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_p\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = c_1\v{b}_1 + c_2\v{b}_2 + \cdots + c_p\v{b}_p \end{equation*}
    The vector of those scalars, denoted as \([\v{x}]_\mathcal{B}\text{,}\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} [\v{x}]_\mathcal{B} = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \\ \vdots \\ c_p \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    and is called the coordinate vector of \(\v{x}\) relative to \(\mathcal{B}\), or the \(\mathcal{B}\)-coordinate vector of \(\v{x}\).
    🔗
    🔗
    You may be wondering why we can uniquely determine the coordinates of a vector relative to a given basis. That is because the scalars are unique for a given vector and a given basis. Feel free to explore this idea if you are interested! The whole point of the proof is based on the fact that basis vectors are linearly independent!
