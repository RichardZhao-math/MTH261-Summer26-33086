    Section 4.3 Diagonalization

    Investigation 4.3.1.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \amp -10 \\ 15 \amp -13 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    (a)

    Find \(A^{25}\) if this is easy to compute. If not, explain why not (and then table it).
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Find \(B^{25}\) if this is easy to compute. If not, explain why not (and then table it).
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 4.3.1. Diagonal Matrices.

    A diagonal matrix is a square matrix in which all elements outside the main diagonal are zero.
    🔗
    🔗
    Diagonal matrices are super nice to work with because
    • It is easy to find their determinants (just multiply the diagonal entries).
      🔗
      🔗
    • It is easy to find their inverses (just take the reciprocals of the diagonal entries).
      🔗
      🔗
    • We can see immediately what they do to the standard basis vectors (just scale them by the diagonal entries).
      🔗
      🔗
    • It is easy to compute the powers of them (just take the powers of the diagonal entries).
      🔗
      🔗
    So the goal of this section is to diagonalize a square matrix (if possible). That is, we want to find a diagonal matrix that is similar to a given matrix.
    🔗

    Definition 4.3.2. Similar Matrices.

    Let \(A\) and \(D\) be \(n \times n\) matrices. Then \(A\) is similar to \(D\) if there exists an invertible matrix \(P\) such that
    \begin{equation*} A = PDP^{-1} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    If you end up studying advanced linear algebra, what is really going on here is that the columns of the matrix \(P\) represent an alternative basis of \(\R^n\text{.}\) If you use that basis instead of the standard basis, the matrix for the corresponding linear transformation actually would be diagonal. This alternative basis is actually made up of eigenvectors and that is what we are going to learn about how.
    🔗
    As far as what this class (MTH 261) concerns, we will focus on the fact that if a matrix \(A\) is similar to a diagonal matrix, then it is still easy to compute powers if we can figure out what the \(D\text{,}\) \(P\text{,}\) and \(P^{-1}\) are.
    🔗
    But how do similar matrices makes it easier to compute powers? Let’s investigate this in the next example.
    🔗

    Example 4.3.3.

    Recall in the previous example, \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 12 \amp -10 \\ 15 \amp -13 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) These two matrices are similar because
    \begin{equation*} B = PAP^{-1} \end{equation*}
    with the matrix \(P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -2 \\ 1 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s first verify it! We can first find the inverse of \(P\) quickly:
    \begin{equation*} P^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det P} \begin{bmatrix} -3 \amp -(-2) \\ -1 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp -2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then we have
    \begin{align*} PAP^{-1} \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -2 \\ 1 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp -2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp 6 \\ 2 \amp 9 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp -2 \\ 1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 12 \amp -10 \\ 15 \amp -13 \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}
    So \(A\) and \(B\) are indeed similar!
    🔗
    Now let’s try to compute \(B^{25}\) by finding \(B^2\) first to find any pattern! Observe that
    \begin{align*} B^2 \amp= (PAP^{-1})(PAP^{-1}) \\ \amp= PA(P^{-1}P)AP^{-1} \\ \amp= PAIAP^{-1} \\ \amp= PAAP^{-1} = PA^2P^{-1} \end{align*}
    If we want to compute \(B^3\text{,}\) we can do the same thing and find that
    \begin{align*} B^3 \amp= (PAP^{-1})(PAP^{-1})(PAP^{-1}) \\ \amp= PA(P^{-1}P)A(P^{-1}P)AP^{-1} \\ \amp= PAIAIPAP^{-1} \\ \amp= PA^3P^{-1} \end{align*}
    So we can find \(B^{25}\) as
    \begin{equation*} B^{25} = PA^{25}P^{-1} \end{equation*}
    We saw that \(A^{25}\) can be computed easily, so we can compute \(B^{25}\) easily as well by just multiplying three matrices together (as opposed to multiplying \(B\) by itself 25 times).
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we have seen how similar matrices (to diagonal matrices) can help us compute powers of matrices, then the next question is: how can we diagonalize a matrix by finding the similar diagonal matrix and the special matrix \(P\text{?}\) It turns out that the key is to find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the matrix!
    🔗
    The process of diagonalization is listed as follows:
    1. Find the eigenvalues of the matrix.
      🔗
    2. For each eigenvalue, find the corresponding eigenvectors. A quick way to do so is to grab the basis vectors in the eigenspace.
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Construct the special matrix \(P\) using the eigenvectors as comlumns, so \(P = \begin{bmatrix} \v{v}_1 \amp \cdots \amp \v{v}_p \end{bmatrix}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Construct the diagonal matrix \(D\) by putting the corresponding eigenvalues of \(A\) on the diagonal entries, up to its multiplicity.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. \(\displaystyle A = PDP^{-1}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Note 4.3.4. But Richard... Why on earth do eigenvalues and eigenvectors have to do with diagonalization?

    Let’s say we have a square matrix \(A\text{.}\) Suppose we let
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} \v{v}_1 \amp \v{v}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \v{v}_n \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{ and } \qquad D = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 \amp 0 \amp \cdots 0 \\ 0 \amp \lambda_2 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \\ \vdots \amp \vdots \amp \ddots \amp \vdots \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    where \(\lambda_i\) and \(\v{v}_i\) are the corresponding eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the matrix \(A\text{.}\) That is, \(A\v{v}_i = \lambda \v{v}_i\) for each \(i\text{.}\) To guarantee that \(P\) is invertible, the columns of \(P\) (aka all the eigenvectors) must be linearly independent. That is why we want to just grab the basis vectors of the eigenspace to ensure the invertibility.
    🔗
    Imagine we want to multiply \(AP\text{.}\) Then we obtain
    \begin{align*} AP \amp= A \begin{bmatrix} \v{v}_1 \amp \v{v}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \v{v}_n \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} A\v{v}_1 \amp A\v{v}_2 \amp \cdots \amp A\v{v}_n \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1\v{v}_1 \amp \lambda_2\v{v}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \lambda_n\v{v}_n \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} \v{v}_1 \amp \v{v}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \v{v}_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 \amp 0 \amp \cdots 0 \\ 0 \amp \lambda_2 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \\ \vdots \amp \vdots \amp \ddots \amp \vdots \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\\ \amp= PD \end{align*}
    So we just showed that \(AP = PD\text{.}\) Since \(P\) is invertible, then \(P^{-1}\) exists, and we can multiply \(P^{-1}\) on the right on both sides of the equation. We obtain
    \begin{equation*} APP^{-1} = PDP^{-1} \qquad \implies \qquad A = PDP^{-1} \end{equation*}
    So yes! \(A\) and \(D\) are similar!
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.3.5.

    Let \(A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \amp 3 \\ -3 \amp -5 \amp -3 \\ 3 \amp 3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Let’s diagonalize this matrix!
    🔗
    We need to find the eigenvalues and its corresponding eigenvectors for the diagonalization. In the last section, we found that the characteristic equation is
    \begin{equation*} (1 - \lambda)(\lambda + 2)^2 = 0 \end{equation*}
    This implies that the eigenvalues are \(\lambda = 1\) (with the multiplicity of \(1\)) and \(\lambda = -2\) (with the multiplicity of \(2\)). Next, we will find the corresponding eigenvectors of these eigenvalues.
    🔗
    Let’s consider the eigenvalue of \(\lambda = 1\text{.}\) Then we want to solve the homogeneous equation \(A - I = 0\text{.}\) The augmented matrix is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 3 \amp 3 \\ -3 \amp -6 \amp -3 \\ 3 \amp 3 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_3\) is the free variable, so the solution, in parametric form, is
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\, , \qquad x_3 \in \R \end{equation*}
    Hence, an eigenvector that spans the eigenspace is \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, let’s consider the eigenvalue of \(\lambda = -2\text{.}\) Then we want to solve the homogeneous equation \(A - I = 0\text{.}\) The augmented matrix is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 3 \amp 3 \\ -3 \amp -3 \amp -3 \\ 3 \amp 3 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_2\) and \(x_3\) are the free variables, so the solution, in parametric form, is
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = x_2 \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_3 \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \, , \qquad x_2, x_3 \in \R \end{equation*}
    Hence, the eigenvectors that spans the eigenspace are \(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now let’s put together the diagonal matrix \(D\) and our special matrix \(P\) for the diagonalization! Our eigenvalues are
    \begin{equation*} \lambda = 1\, ,\, -2\, , \, -2 \end{equation*}
    and their corresponding basis eigenvectors are
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\, , \, \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\, , \, \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then our diagonal matrix \(D\) and our special matrix \(P\) is
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \\ -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{ and } \qquad D = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    This is a good place to call it good for diagonalization since we found \(P\) and \(D\text{.}\) We can next verify it by making sure \(A\) is indeed \(PDP^{-1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We can find \(P^{-1}\) by reducing \(\begin{bmatrix} P \mid I \end{bmatrix} \) to \(\begin{bmatrix} I \mid P^{-1} \end{bmatrix} \text{.}\)
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \amp \mid \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp \mid \amp \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp \mid \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \mid \amp 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp \mid \amp 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp \mid \amp -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(P^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now we can verify that
    \begin{align*} PDP^{-1} \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \\ -1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp -2 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 2 \amp 3 \\ -1 \amp -1 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 3 \amp 3 \\ -3 \amp -5 \amp -3 \\ 3 \amp 3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp= A \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    So to diagonalize a matrix, we just need to find all the eigenvalues and its corresponding basis eigenvectors. Then we put things into the right place in the right order.
    🔗

    Example 4.3.6.

    Let’s diagonalize this matrix \(B = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \amp 1 \amp -4 \\ 6 \amp 2 \amp -6 \\ 6 \amp 1 \amp -5 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We want to first find its eigenvalues. That is, we want to solve the characteristic equation \(\det (B - \lambda I) = 0\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \det(B - \lambda I) \amp= \begin{vmatrix} 5 - \lambda \amp 1 \amp -4 \\ 6 \amp 2 - \lambda \amp -6 \\ 6 \amp 1 \amp -5 - \lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp= (5 - \lambda) \begin{vmatrix} 2 - \lambda \amp -6 \\ 1 \amp -5 - \lambda \end{vmatrix} - 1 \begin{vmatrix} 6 \amp -6 \\ 6 \amp -5 - \lambda \end{vmatrix} + (-4) \begin{vmatrix} 6 \amp 2 - \lambda \\ 6 \amp 1 \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp= (5 - \lambda) \big((2 - \lambda)(-5 - \lambda) - (-6)(1)\big) - \big(6(-5 - \lambda) - (6)(-6)\big) - 4\big((6)(1) - 6(2 - \lambda)\big) \\ \amp= (5 - \lambda)(\lambda^2 + 3\lambda - 4) - (6 - 6\lambda) - 4(6\lambda - 6) \\ \amp= (-\lambda^3 + 2\lambda^2 + 19\lambda - 20) + 6\lambda - 6 - 24\lambda + 24 \\ \amp= -\lambda^3 + 2\lambda^2 + \lambda - 2 \\ \amp= -\lambda^2(\lambda - 2) + 1(\lambda - 2) \\ \amp= (1 - \lambda^2)(\lambda - 2) \\ \amp= (1 - \lambda)(1 + \lambda)(\lambda - 2) \end{align*}
    Now we solve the characteristic equation
    \begin{equation*} (1 + \lambda)(1 - \lambda)(\lambda - 2) = 0 \end{equation*}
    to obtain the eigenvalues of \(-1\text{,}\) \(1\text{,}\) and \(2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s consider the eigenvalue of \(\lambda = -1\text{.}\) Then we want to solve the homogeneous equation \(A + I = 0\text{.}\) The augmented matrix is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 6 \amp 1 \amp -4 \\ 6 \amp 3 \amp -6 \\ 6 \amp 1 \amp -4 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_3\) is the free variable, so the solution, in parametric form, is
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \, , \qquad x_3 \in \R \end{equation*}
    To avoid fractions, we can choose \(x_3 = 2\text{.}\) Hence, an eigenvector that spans the eigenspace \(E_{-1}\) is \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Next, let’s consider the eigenvalue of of \(\lambda = 1\text{.}\) Then we want to solve the homogeneous equation \(A - I = 0\text{.}\) The augmented matrix is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \amp 1 \amp -4 \\ 6 \amp 1 \amp -6 \\ 6 \amp 1 \amp -6 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_3\) is the free variable, so the solution, in parametric form, is
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\, , \qquad x_3 \in \R \end{equation*}
    Hence, an eigenvector that spans the eigenspace \(E_1\) is \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, let’s consider the eigenvalue of \(\lambda = 2\text{.}\) Then we want to solve the homogeneous equation \(A - 2I = 0\text{.}\) The augmented matrix is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 1 \amp -4 \\ 6 \amp 0 \amp -6 \\ 6 \amp 1 \amp -7 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_3\) is the free variable, so the solution, in parametric form, is
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\, , \qquad x_3 \in \R \end{equation*}
    Hence, an eigenvector that spans the eigenspace \(E_2\) is \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now we put together the diagonal matrix \(D\) and our special matrix \(P\text{.}\) Our eigenvalues are
    \begin{equation*} \lambda = 1\, ,\, 2\, , \, -1 \end{equation*}
    and their corresponding basis eigenvectors are
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\, , \, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\, , \, \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then our diagonal matrix \(D\) and our special matrix \(P\) is
    \begin{equation*} P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 2 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix} \qquad \text{ and } \qquad D = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 2 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Feel free to verify our answer by computing \(PDP^{-1}\) and see if we get \(B\) out of it.
    🔗
    🔗
    You may observe that from the previous two examples, the multiplicity of an eigenvalue matches up with the dimension of the eigenspace. This is the condition for a matrix to be diagonalizable. That is, if the multiplicity does not match with the dimension, the matrix is not diagonalizable.
    🔗

    Example 4.3.7.

    Let’s say our matrix \(C = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and we want to diagonalize it.
    🔗
    We will first find the eigenvalues of \(C\text{.}\) The characteristic polynomial is
    \begin{align*} \det(C - \lambda I) \amp= \begin{vmatrix} -\lambda \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp -\lambda \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 0 \amp -\lambda \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp= -\lambda \begin{vmatrix} -\lambda \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp -\lambda \end{vmatrix} - 1 \begin{vmatrix} 3 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp -\lambda \end{vmatrix} + 0 \begin{vmatrix} 3 \amp -\lambda \\ 2 \amp 0 \end{vmatrix} \\ \amp= -\lambda \big((-\lambda)(-\lambda) - (1)(0)\big) - 1\big((3)(-\lambda) - (1)(2)\big) + 0 \\ \amp= -\lambda(\lambda^2 - 0) - (-3\lambda - 2) \\ \amp= -\lambda^3 + 3\lambda + 2 \end{align*}
    We can factor the characteristic polynomial as follows:
    \begin{align*} -\lambda^3 + 3\lambda + 2 \amp= -\lambda^3 + \lambda + 2\lambda + 2 \\ \amp= -\lambda(\lambda^2 - 1) + 2(\lambda + 1) \\ \amp= -\lambda(\lambda - 1)(\lambda + 1) + 2(\lambda + 1) \\ \amp= (\lambda + 1) \big(-\lambda(\lambda - 1) + 2\big) \\ \amp= (\lambda + 1)(-\lambda^2 + \lambda + 2) \\ \amp= -(\lambda + 1)(\lambda^2 - \lambda - 2) \\ \amp= -(\lambda + 1)(\lambda - 2)(\lambda + 1) \\ \amp= -(\lambda - 2)(\lambda + 1)^2 \end{align*}
    So we can solve the characteristic equation
    \begin{equation*} -(\lambda - 2)(\lambda + 1)^2 = 0 \end{equation*}
    and obtain the eigenvalues of \(2\) (with the multiplicity of \(1\)) and \(-1\) (with the multiplicity of \(2\)).
    🔗
    NOTE: Feel free to use technology to help you solve the characteristic equation. Remember that we should obtain the number of solutions matching up with the degree of the equation, including multiplicity, guaranteed by the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra.
    🔗
    Next, let’s find the corresponding eigenvectors for these eigenvalues. We will start with \(\lambda = 2\text{.}\) We want to solve the homogeneous equation \(C - 2I = 0\text{.}\) The augmented matrix is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp -2 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 0 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -2 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_3\) is the free variable, so the solution in parametric form is
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\, , \qquad x_3 \in \R \end{equation*}
    Hence, an eigenvector that spans this eigenspace is \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now let’s consider the eigenvalue \(\lambda = -1\text{.}\) We want to solve the homogeneous equation \(C + I = 0\text{.}\) The augmented matrix is
    \begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 3 \amp 1 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp -2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \sim \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -\frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Observe that \(x_3\) is the only free variable. The solution in parametric form is
    \begin{equation*} \v{x} = x_3 \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \, , \qquad x_3 \in \R \end{equation*}
    To avoid fractions, we can choose \(x_3 = 2\text{,}\) which gives us the eigenvector \(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\) that spans the eigenspace.
    🔗
    Wait a minute! We have a problem. For the \(3 \times 3\) matrix \(C\) to be diagonalizable, we need \(3\) linearly independent eigenvectors to form our invertible matrix \(P\text{.}\) However, the eigenvalue \(\lambda = -1\) has an algebraic multiplicity of \(2\text{,}\) but its eigenspace is spanned just by one basis vector of \(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{...}\) We don’t have enough basis vectors to construct our special matrix \(P\text{...}\)
    🔗
    Therefore, the matrix \(C\) is not diagonalizable!
