    Section 5.1 Inner Product, Length, and Orthogonality

    Subsection Length of a Vector

    The length of a vector, (aka the magnitude or the norm), can be computed using the Pythagorean Theorem (in \(\R^2\) or \(\R^3\) for possible visualization).
    🔗

    Definition 5.1.1. Length of a Vector.

    Let \(\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix}\in \R^n\text{.}\) Its length, denoted \(\|\v{v}\|\text{,}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \|\v{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots v_n^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Using this formula, we can define the distance between two vectors, which is essentially the difference between the tip of two vectors.
    🔗

    Definition 5.1.2. Distance between two Vectors.

    Let \(\v{v}, \v{w} \in \R^n\) such that \(\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\v{w} = \begin{bmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \\ \vdots \\ w_n \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) The distance between them, denoted \(\dist(\v{v}, \v{w})\text{,}\) is
    \begin{equation*} \dist(\v{v}, \v{w}) = \sqrt{\lp v_1 - w_1 \rp^2 + \lp v_2 - w_2 \rp^2 + \cdots + \lp v_n - w_n \rp^2} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection Inner Product

    You may recall that you learned the concept called the inner product before.
    🔗

    Definition 5.1.3. Inner Product.

    The inner product \(\boldsymbol{\v{v}, \v{w}}\) of two vectors
    \begin{equation*} \v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots v_n \end{bmatrix} \, , \qquad \v{w} = \begin{bmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \\ \vdots w_n \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}
    is the scalar defined by
    \begin{equation*} \v{v}, \v{w} = v_1 w_1 + v_2 w_2 + \cdots + v_n w_n \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(\v{v}, \v{w} \in \R^n\) be two vectors defined as
    \begin{equation*} \v{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{pmatrix} \, , \qquad \v{w} = \begin{pmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \\ \vdots \\ w_n \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    Then the standard inner product of \(\v{v}\) and \(\v{w}\) , denoted by \(\v{v}, \v{w} \text{,}\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} \v{v}, \v{w} = \v{v}^T \v{w} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \amp v_2 \amp \cdots \amp v_n \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \\ \vdots \\ w_n \end{pmatrix} = v_1 w_1 + v_2 w_2 + \cdots + v_n w_n \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Observe that we can find the inner product of two vectors by multiplying their corresponding components and adding the results.
    🔗

    Example 5.1.4.

    Compute the following inner products:
    1. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 3 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} = 1\cdot 4 + 2\cdot 3 + 1\cdot 5 = 4 + 6 + 5 = 15\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \begin{bmatrix} -3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\cdot \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} = -3\cdot 4 + 1\cdot 6 = -12 + 6 = -6\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Remember that the inner product of two vectors results in a scalar (a real number) and not another vector. The inner product has several useful properties that we summarize in the following theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.5. Properties of the Inner Product.

    1. \(\v{0}\cdot \v{v} = 0\text{,}\) where \(\v{0}\) is the zero vector.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Commutativity: \(\v{v}\cdot \v{w} = \v{w} \cdot \v{v}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Pulling out scalars: \((\lambda \v{v}) \cdot \v{w} = \v{v}, xxzx``\lambda \v{w} = \lambda \v{v}\cdot \v{w}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Distributive Law: \(\v{u}\cdot \v{v} + \v{w} = \v{u}\cdot \v{v} + \v{u}\cdot \v{w} \)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Relation with length: \(\v{v}, \v{v} = \|\v{v}\|^2\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof(ish) of the above theorem.
    The proofs of the properties are pretty straightforward using the definition of the inner product. Richard will prove some of them in \(\R^3\) and leave the rest as exercises for you to verify (especially if you are thinking about majoring in math). You may also want to see if you are convinced that Richard’s proofs can be generalized to \(\R^n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Let \(\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{equation*} \v{0}, \v{v} = 0 \cdot v_1 + 0 \cdot v_2 + 0 \cdot v_3 = 0 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Let \(\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\v{w} = \begin{bmatrix} w_1 \\ w_2 \\ w_3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{align*} \v{v}, \v{w} \amp= v_1 w_1 + v_2 w_2 + v_3 w_3 \\ \amp= w_1 v_1 + w_2 v_2 + w_3 v_3 \\ \amp= \v{w}, \v{v} \end{align*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. The proof is left as an exercise for the reader.
      🔗
      🔗
    4. The proof is left as an exercise for the reader.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Let \(\v{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\) Then
      \begin{equation*} \v{v}, \v{v} = v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2 = \|\v{v}\|^2 \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Using these properties, we can prove some cool results! For example, do you know that the sum of the squares of the lengths of the four sides of a parallelogram is equal to the sum of the squares of the lengths of its two diagonals? This is also known as the parallelogram law.
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.6. Parallelogram Law.

    \begin{equation*} \|\v{u} + \v{v}\|^2 + \|\v{u} - \v{v}\|^2 = 2\|\v{u}\|^2 + 2\|\v{v}\|^2 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 5.1.7.

    Prove the parallelogram law using the properties of the inner product. The diagram below shows a parallelogram formed by the vectors \(\v{u}\) and \(\v{v}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hint.
    The parallelogram has two diagonals with the lengths of \(\|\v{u} + \v{v}\|\) and \(\|\v{u} - \v{v}\|\text{.}\) Also, the sides have lengths of \(\|\v{u}\|\) and \(\|\v{v}\|\text{,}\) respectively.
    🔗
    We are learning about the inner product so let’s get some inner products involved! One of the inner product properties states that \(\|\v{v}\|^2 = \v{v}, \v{v}\) so converting all the lengths into inner products is a good start!
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    \begin{align*} \|\v{u} + \v{v}\|^2 + \|\v{u} - \v{v}\|^2 \amp= \v{u} + \v{v}, \v{u} + \v{v} + \la \v{u} - \v{v}, \v{u} - \v{v} \\ \amp= \v{u}, \v{u} + 2 \v{u}, \v{v} + \v{v}, \v{v} + \v{u}, \v{u} - 2\v{u}, \v{v} + \v{v}, \v{v}\\ \amp= 2 \v{u}, \v{u} + 2 \v{v}, \v{v} \\ \amp= 2\|\v{u}\|^2 + 2\|\v{v}\|^2 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Now that we know what the inner product is and some of its properties, we will next explore why we care about it.
    🔗
    🔗
